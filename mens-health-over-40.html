Men's Health Over 40: A Practical Playbook

Turning 40 isn't a cliff. It's more like a gradual hill that gets steeper if you ignore it. The changes that happen in your 40s, 50s, and 60s aren't mysterious. They're well-documented, predictable, and largely manageable. The men who age well aren't lucky. They're informed and consistent.

This guide covers the practical realities of men's health after 40: what changes, why it changes, and what you can do about each one. No hype, no fear-mongering. Just the information you need to make good decisions.

What Changes After 40 (and What to Do About It)

1. Hormonal Shifts

Testosterone drops about 1-2% per year after 30. By 50, most men have 20-30% less than their peak. This affects energy, body composition, mood, sexual function, and cognitive performance. You can't stop the decline, but you can slow it significantly.

What helps: Resistance training 3-4 times weekly is the single most effective natural testosterone supporter. Sleep 7-9 hours. Manage stress. Maintain healthy body fat. Supplements like tribulus terrestris and fenugreek provide additional support. For a detailed breakdown, see our testosterone support guide.

2. Body Composition

Men lose 3-5% of muscle mass per decade after 30, a condition called sarcopenia. At the same time, visceral fat (the deep belly fat around organs) increases. This combination raises the risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.

What helps: Resistance training is non-negotiable. It's the only intervention that simultaneously builds muscle and reduces visceral fat. Protein intake matters too: aim for 0.7-1g per pound of body weight daily. Reduce processed foods and excess sugar. For more on this, see our male vitality guide.

3. Prostate Health

By 60, over half of men have some degree of BPH (enlarged prostate). By 80, it's 90%. BPH causes urinary frequency, urgency, weak flow, and nighttime bathroom trips that destroy sleep quality. Prostate cancer is also the second most common cancer in men.

What helps: Regular prostate screenings starting at 50 (or 40 with risk factors). Saw palmetto has clinical support for mild to moderate BPH symptoms. Maintain a healthy weight. Exercise regularly. Limit alcohol. Get the full picture in our prostate health guide.

4. Cardiovascular Health

Heart disease is the leading cause of death in men. Risk factors accumulate over decades: high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking, obesity, and sedentary lifestyle. Blood vessels become less elastic with age, and plaque buildup accelerates.

What helps: Regular cardiovascular exercise (150+ minutes per week of moderate intensity). Manage blood pressure and cholesterol. Don't smoke. Maintain healthy weight. Omega-3 fatty acids support heart health. Ingredients that improve vascular function (like epimedium) provide additional cardiovascular support.

5. Sleep Quality

Deep sleep decreases with age. Men over 40 spend less time in the restorative stages of sleep. Since testosterone production occurs primarily during deep sleep, poor sleep directly suppresses testosterone. Poor sleep also increases cortisol, which further suppresses testosterone and promotes fat storage.

What helps: Consistent sleep schedule. Cool, dark bedroom. Limit screens before bed. Avoid caffeine after 2 PM. Exercise (but not close to bedtime). Magnesium supplementation supports sleep quality. Address prostate issues that cause nighttime bathroom trips.

6. Mental Health and Cognitive Function

Depression rates in men increase after 40. Social isolation, career stress, relationship changes, and hormonal shifts all contribute. Cognitive processing speed begins declining around 45. Memory issues that seem minor can be early warning signs.

What helps: Social connection. Regular exercise (which has anti-depressant effects). Stress management. Adequate sleep. Ingredients that support dopamine and mood (like mucuna pruriens) can help. Don't hesitate to seek professional help for persistent mood changes.

7. Sexual Health

About 30% of men over 50 report some degree of erectile difficulty. By 60, it's 50%. ED is partly vascular, partly hormonal, and partly neurological. It can also be an early warning sign of cardiovascular disease.

What helps: Cardiovascular exercise improves blood flow. Maintaining healthy weight and hormone levels supports sexual function. Natural ingredients like epimedium (which acts as a mild PDE5 inhibitor) provide additional support. For a full discussion, see our natural male enhancement guide.

The Exercise Prescription for Men Over 40

Exercise isn't optional after 40. It's medicine. But the type and intensity of exercise matters more than it did at 25.

Resistance Training (3-4 times per week)

This is the most important type of exercise for men over 40. It builds and maintains muscle mass, supports testosterone production, strengthens bones, improves insulin sensitivity, and reduces visceral fat. Focus on compound movements: squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows, overhead press. Aim for 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps with challenging weights.

Cardiovascular Exercise (150+ minutes per week)

Walking, cycling, swimming, or jogging. Moderate intensity where you can still hold a conversation. Cardiovascular exercise supports heart health, improves blood flow, aids recovery between resistance training sessions, and supports mental health.

Mobility and Flexibility (daily)

Stretching, yoga, or dedicated mobility work. Flexibility decreases with age, and stiffness increases the risk of injury during exercise. Even 10 minutes of daily stretching makes a significant difference over months.

Nutrition After 40

Metabolism slows after 40, but not as dramatically as most people think. The bigger issue is that men tend to eat the same amount while becoming less active, leading to gradual fat gain. Here are the nutrition priorities:

  • Protein: At least 0.7-1g per pound of body weight. This supports muscle maintenance and repair. Many men over 40 don't eat enough protein, which accelerates muscle loss.
  • Fiber: 25-35g daily. Supports digestive health, helps manage weight, and reduces cardiovascular risk. Most men get about 15g.
  • Healthy fats: Olive oil, nuts, fatty fish, avocados. Essential for hormone production (testosterone is made from cholesterol).
  • Micronutrients: Vitamin D (most men are deficient), magnesium (supports 300+ enzymatic reactions), zinc (essential for testosterone production), and omega-3s (anti-inflammatory, supports heart and brain health).
  • Limit: Processed foods, excess sugar, excessive alcohol. These promote inflammation, disrupt sleep, and suppress testosterone.

The Screening Checklist

Men over 40 should stay on top of these screenings and checkups:

  • Blood pressure: At least once a year. High blood pressure is the "silent killer" because it usually has no symptoms.
  • Cholesterol panel: Every 4-6 years (more often if elevated).
  • Blood glucose/A1C: Every 3 years starting at 45 (earlier if overweight).
  • Prostate screening: PSA test and digital rectal exam starting at 50 (or 40 with risk factors).
  • Testosterone levels: If experiencing symptoms of low testosterone.
  • Skin cancer screening: Annual dermatologist visit.
  • Eye exam: Every 2 years after 40.
  • Dental cleaning: Every 6 months.
  • Mental health check: If experiencing persistent mood changes, anxiety, or depression symptoms.

Building a Sustainable Routine

The best health plan is the one you'll actually follow. Here's a realistic daily framework for men over 40:

  • Morning: Consistent wake time. Take Manjuba Drops with breakfast. 10 minutes of stretching or light movement.
  • Midday: 30-45 minute resistance training session (3-4 times per week) or cardiovascular exercise. On non-training days, a 20-30 minute walk.
  • Evening: Protein-rich dinner. Limit screens 1 hour before bed. Consistent bedtime. 7-9 hours of sleep.
  • Weekly: 2-3 resistance training sessions, 2-3 cardio sessions, daily stretching. One rest day.

Start where you are. If you're currently sedentary, don't try to go from zero to six gym sessions per week. Start with three sessions, build the habit, then add more. Consistency over intensity wins every time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the biggest health risks for men over 40?

The primary health concerns for men over 40 include cardiovascular disease, prostate issues, type 2 diabetes, hormonal decline, and mental health challenges. Regular checkups, healthy lifestyle habits, and appropriate supplementation can help manage these risks.

How much exercise should men over 40 get?

The WHO recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, plus 2 or more days of muscle-strengthening activities. For men over 40, resistance training is particularly important because it supports muscle mass, bone density, and hormone production.

Should men over 40 take supplements?

Most men over 40 benefit from at least some supplementation. Common choices include vitamin D, omega-3s, magnesium, and zinc. For male-specific concerns like energy and vitality, supplements like Manjuba Drops provide targeted support with clinically studied ingredients.
