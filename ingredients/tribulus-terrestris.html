Tribulus Terrestris: How This Ancient Herb Supports Male Hormones

Tribulus terrestris shows up in almost every male vitality supplement on the market. Some of that is hype. Some of it is earned. The truth sits somewhere in the middle, and understanding what tribulus actually does — and what it does not do — helps you evaluate whether it belongs in a formula like Manjuba Drops.

Let's break down the science, the traditional use, and why Manjuba Drops includes this ingredient.

What Is Tribulus Terrestris?

Tribulus terrestris is a flowering plant in the family Zygophyllaceae. It grows low to the ground and produces small, spiky fruit that has earned it less flattering names like puncture vine and caltrop. The plant is native to warm, dry regions of southern Europe, the Middle East, North Africa, and India.

The parts used medicinally are the fruit, leaf, and root. In Ayurvedic medicine, tribulus has been used for thousands of years under the name gokshura. Traditionally, it was prescribed for urinary problems, kidney stones, and male reproductive health. In Bulgarian and Eastern European sports medicine, tribulus became popular in the 1980s when Olympic athletes from Bulgaria claimed it boosted their performance.

The active compounds in tribulus are called steroidal saponins, with protodioscin being the most studied. These saponins are what give tribulus its biological activity. A good tribulus supplement should be standardized for saponin content — without that standardization, you might be getting mostly plant fiber.

How Tribulus Supports Hormonal Balance

Here is the part that trips people up. Tribulus terrestris does not directly increase testosterone. That is the most common misconception about this ingredient. If you see a supplement claiming tribulus "boosts testosterone by 400%," that is marketing, not science.

What tribulus does is support the hormonal system upstream. It appears to influence luteinizing hormone (LH) release from the pituitary gland. LH is the signal that tells the Leydig cells in the testes to produce testosterone. By supporting healthy LH signaling, tribulus helps the body maintain its own natural testosterone production.

There is also evidence that protodioscin may interact with androgen receptors and influence how the body uses the testosterone it already produces. A 2007 study in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition found that tribulus supplementation for 20 days increased free testosterone levels in young men during a resistance training program. The increase was modest but measurable.

Tribulus may also support hormone balance by interacting with estrogen receptors. Some research suggests that certain saponins in tribulus can bind to estrogen receptor beta, which may help modulate the ratio of testosterone to estrogen. This is relevant for men over 40, when estrogen levels tend to rise while testosterone falls.

What the Clinical Studies Show

The clinical picture for tribulus is mixed, and being honest about that matters. Some studies show clear benefits, others show nothing. The context of the study — who the participants are, what doses they took, and how long the study lasted — makes a big difference.

A 2016 meta-analysis in Complementary Therapies in Medicine reviewed 12 randomized controlled trials and found that tribulus terrestris significantly improved sexual function in men. The studies that showed the best results tended to use doses between 250mg and 750mg daily for at least 90 days.

A 2014 study in Andrologia tested tribulus on 180 men with mild to moderate erectile dysfunction. After 12 weeks, the men taking tribulus showed improvements in erectile function scores that were significantly better than placebo. Importantly, this was in men who did not respond fully to sildenafil alone, suggesting tribulus works through mechanisms beyond simple PDE5 inhibition.

In healthy men with normal testosterone levels, tribulus tends to have less dramatic effects on hormone levels. This makes sense — if your hormonal system is already functioning well, there is less room for improvement. The benefits are more noticeable in men experiencing age-related decline or hormonal imbalance.

Safety and Side Effects

Tribulus terrestris has been used in traditional medicine for centuries without major safety concerns. The most commonly reported side effect is mild gastrointestinal discomfort — occasional stomach upset or nausea, which usually resolves as the body adjusts to the supplement.

There are some situations where caution makes sense. Men with prostate cancer or other hormone-sensitive cancers should avoid tribulus without medical supervision. The herb may affect hormone levels in ways that could interact with hormone therapy. Men taking medications for blood pressure or diabetes should also check with their doctor, as tribulus may interact with those drugs.

Pregnant or nursing women should not take tribulus. As with any supplement, if you have a pre-existing medical condition or take prescription medication, talk to your healthcare provider before adding it to your routine.
